The Diabetes Dilemma

September 21, 2024

Diabetes mellitus is a disease that occurs when the pancreas cannot sufficiently regulate an animal‚Äôs blood sugar- leading to excessive glucose in the blood that is unable to be used by the cells for energy. It is relatively common and affects 1 in every 300 patients, which are mostly middle-aged to elderly dogs and cats.

There are three types:
Type I: Insulin-producing cells are almost destroyed.
Type II: Insulin-producing cells are somewhat present, but insulin is insufficient.
Type III: Insulin resistance caused by other hormones, pregnancy, and/or tumors

Clinical signs:

‚Ä¢ Increased appetite as the body cannot use glucose for energy.
‚Ä¢ Weight loss as the body breaks down fat and protein stores to compensate for the energy loss.

The proper treatment for diabetes mellitus differs based on the type. Type I is treated with two insulin injections QD. Type II and Type III are treated with oral medications, such as corticosteroids and progestogens. For all types, pets should also be fed a nutritious diet.

Diabetes can be diagnosed by a veterinarian given the typical symptoms being displayed by the animal and excessive/present glucose in the blood and urine upon lab tests. To prevent diabetes, owners should provide their pets with a healthy, well-balanced diet that is high in protein and low in carbohydrates in order to maintain a blood sugar level that is steady, as well as promote regular exercise.

Navigating the complexities of diabetes can feel overwhelming, but understanding the condition is the first step toward helping our cats and dogs lead healthy lives.

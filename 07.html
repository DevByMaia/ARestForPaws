Why do Cats Purr?

February 17, 2024

If you’ve ever had a cat, you know that purring is their favorite background noise, like the soundtrack they chose for themselves. Cats purr while nestled next to you, while curled in their favorite spot, and, even while perched on top of fresh laundry, claiming it as their own.

The common belief is that cats purr because they’re content. But ask any cat owner, and they’ll tell you the truth: cats also purr while biting your arm, scratching up your leather couch, or plotting who knows what from atop the kitchen counter. It’s unsettling when your pet seems to be communicating all sorts of conflicting emotions through the same, soothing hum. And so we ask—why do cats purr, and what are they trying to tell us?

The Science of the Purr

Research tells us that a purr is a vibration created by rapid twitching of the muscles around a cat’s voice box. It’s a reflexive action that starts when they’re kittens, bonding them to their mother and saying, “Hi, I’m here.” Cats learn to purr before they learn to meow, which makes purring something of a primal language, like the Morse code of feline communication. But this code has multiple interpretations.

The Comfort Purr

Of course, we have the “comfort” purr, the one that makes everyone feel good inside. Scientists suggest that comfort purring is as much for the cat as it is for us. It’s their way of saying, “All is right in my world,” which means they’re well-fed, warm, and not in the presence of any offending dogs or children who have violated their personal space. And while it does mean they’re happy, this purr isn’t really about you; it’s more of a sonic hug cats give themselves. If it happens to make us think our cats are lavishing us with affection, well, all the better for them.

The “Everything is NOT Okay” Purr

Then there’s the purr that leaves you deeply unnerved—the stress purr. Cats have been known to purr while visiting the vet, while recovering from injuries, or even during stressful moments, like being surrounded by children who want to “pet” them like a ragdoll. These purrs are often mistaken for contentment, but in reality, they’re a coping mechanism.

Imagine if, every time you had a bad day, you just hummed to yourself in a sort of calming lullaby. People might wonder about you, but you’d probably feel better. This is the cat’s stress purr. It’s their way of self-soothing, almost like a nervous tic. So, next time you’re at the vet with a cat who’s purring louder than usual, don’t feel flattered; feel apologetic. Your cat’s telling you, in the most passive-aggressive way possible, that you’ve put them in a situation they’d rather not be in.

The Manipulation Purr (“Feed Me Now”)

Cats have perfected the art of manipulation, and they’ve managed to incorporate it into their purrs. There’s a special “feed me” purr that’s a bit higher in pitch, and somehow, it tugs at a part of your brain that demands immediate attention. It’s a purr that’s been described as having the same frequency as a baby’s cry, scientifically crafted to make you drop everything and reach for the nearest can of tuna. Cats, it turns out, are adept at making us feel like we’re spoiling them out of choice when, in fact, we’re mere puppets responding to their calculated, high-frequency purr.

The Healing Purr: Feline Medicine at 25 Hz

Here’s where things get intriguing, though: cats actually seem to use their purring as a form of self-care. Cats purr at a frequency of 25 to 150 Hz, which has been shown to promote tissue regeneration and pain relief in other animals. They’re basically walking, purring versions of those high-tech body recovery machines you see in sci-fi movies.

Although scientists theorize that cats purr to heal themselves, they can’t explain why a cat purring on your chest after a hard day makes you feel better, too. Studies suggest there may be a low-key therapeutic effect to their purr, like a living white noise machine that also provides warm cuddles. It’s almost like cats have found a way to make us feel like they’re giving us something valuable, like they're life coaches who occasionally hiss at us. It’s purring for profit, but the returns are worth it.

In the End, a Purr is as Mysterious as the Cat Itself

The purr remains as much of an enigma as the cat itself. It can mean they’re comfortable, terrified, hungry, or on the verge of hatching a new scheme to knock over your drink. What’s clear, though, is that cats purr with purpose. Each little vibration is part of an intricately coded message that only they truly understand.

So, why do cats purr? The short answer is that it’s a complex symphony of contentment, manipulation, and occasional fear—all humming along at frequencies designed to soothe themselves and occasionally confuse us. But we keep indulging them, assuming each purr is a tiny gift, a show of affection, or, at the very least, a sign that maybe the cat likes us.
